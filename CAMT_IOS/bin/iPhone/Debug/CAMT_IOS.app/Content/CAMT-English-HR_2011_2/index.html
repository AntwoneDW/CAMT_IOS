equipment.

Control Circuits
The link between the load and the input power. Control
circuits start, stop, and control the operation of a load.
These circuits usually contain one or more control devices
including switches and thermostats that apply or remove
power from the loads. Control circuits usually operate at

smaller and lighter wiring.

Load Circuits
Circuits that energize loads in air conditioning systems.
Loads are devices that convert electrical energy to another
type of energy such as heat or mechanical motion. In
this process, loads consume power. Compressor motors
and fan motors are loads found in air conditioning units.
Because the load circuits are both energized and operate
at the input voltage level, they are often called the high-
voltage circuits.

Seasonal Energy Efficiency Ratio (SEER)
The SEER rating is the British Thermal Units (BTUs) of
cooling output during a typical cooling season divided
by the total electric energy input in watt hours during the
same period. The higher the SEER rating, the more efficient
the unit is to operate—meaning, there is less electricity used
to accomplish the same task.

Electrical storage devices that help to overcome the torque

HVAC Maintenance and Repair
Sub-Cooling
Heat removed from the refrigerant after it has changed
(condensed) from high pressure/temperature vapor to high
pressure/temperature liquid inside the condensing coils.
The sub-cooling method is used to check and adjust the
charge in air conditioning systems that use thermostatic
expansion valves (TXVs) and newer, environmentally friendly
refrigerants such as R-410A.
Superheat
The heat added by the interior ambient air to the refrigerant
after it has changed from liquid to a vapor inside the
evaporator. Calculating the amount of superheat in the
system determines if it is properly charged with refrigerant.
Supply Air
Located on the discharge side of the evaporating coil,
the supply delivers cool air to the apartment. The supply
includes the building’s plenum, supply duct, branch ducts,
and wall registers.

Gas furnaces use a principle called combustion to create
energy and heat. During combustion, fuel ignited by a

Gas flows to the burners through a pipe called the

Heat
When heat energy is applied to a substance, the molecules
of that substance start to move more rapidly. They collide
with each other, and this friction causes the temperature
of the substance to rise. Conversely, when the heat energy
is removed, the molecules slow down and the amount of
friction decreases.

Heat Transfer
Heat can be transferred from one place to another by three
methods: conduction in solids, convection of fluids (liquids
or gases), and radiation through anything that will allow
radiation to pass. Heating systems use a convection transfer
method to transfer heat.
Hydronic Heating Systems
Hydronic heating systems rely on circulating hot water
to deliver heat to the locations where heating is desired.
Instead of using large air ducts, such as furnaces, water
is heated at one location and then pumped to other
places via piping. The heat is transferred to the space by
heat exchangers located within the space. These heat
exchangers are usually radiators or baseboards and are also
called terminal units.
