Air Conditioners with SEER 13 Ratings
In this part of the training, you’ll learn about the latest generation of air conditioners, those
with a Seasonal Energy Efficiency Ratio (SEER) of 13.
Introduction
The efficiency of an air conditioner is determined by its Seasonal Energy Efficiency Ratio (SEER) , which is defined by the Air
Conditioning, Heating, and Refrigeration Institute.
The higher the SEER, the more energy efficient an air conditioner is.
How SEER Is Calculated
The SEER rating is the British Thermal Units (BTUs) of cooling output during a typical cooling season divided by the total
electric energy input in watt hours during the same period.
For example, a 5000 BTU air-conditioning unit, with a SEER rating of 10, operating for a total of 1000 hours during an annual
cooling season (e.g., 8 hours per day for 125 days) would provide an annual total cooling output of 5000 BTUH (British
Thermal Units per Hour) times 1000 hours of operation, which equals 5,000,000 BTU`s during the cooling season.
The higher the SEER rating, the more efficient the unit is to operate—that is, there is less electricity used to accomplish the
same task. An air conditioner with a 10 SEER rating and one with a 13 SEER rating will both cool the room using the same
5,000,000 BTUs during the cooling season. But one, the 13 SEER unit, is going to use much less electricity to get the
job done.
13 SEER Units Are Larger
When you compare an air conditioning system with a 10 SEER rating to one with a 13 SEER rating, you’ll immediately notice
that the 13 SEER’s evaporator and condensing units are larger.
But why?
For the 13 SEER unit to use less energy, it must get rid of its heat more efficiently. To accomplish this, manufacturers have
increased the size of the condenser coil to allow for more tubing. The more loops in the tubing for the refrigerant to flow
through, the more heat it is able to get rid of, and thus, the efficiencies are greater.
13 SEER Units Operate at Less Pressure, Too
Moreover, a 13 SEER unit’s ability to release more heat from the refrigerant will also reduce its operating pressure. Normal
operating pressure for a 10 SEER unit on an average day may be 72 pounds per square inch (psi) at the suction line, and 270
head pressure; on a 13 SEER unit the suction pressure may be 66psi, with the head pressure at 230. It is a direct relationship
to pressure/temperature slide. As one changes, the other will, too.
This is important to remember because matching a 13 SEER condensing unit to a 10 SEER evaporator requires several
components to be in order:
  • If the liquid line set is smaller than 3/8”, you’ll have trouble establishing proper pressures and superheat. Remember:
    • we’ve reduced the pressure and temperature on the 13 SEER condenser, but we still need to move the same amount of
    refrigerant through the system. Trying to push it at less pressure through the small liquid line simply won’t work.
  • The evaporator and fan also have to be clean so that no reduction in airflow takes place. The units were designed to
    • operate with a set airflow, and reducing it will change the refrigerant’s ability to perform its duty. Reduced airflow will also
    affect the split temperature in the unit and could generate a false reading in the superheat or subcooling. The end result?
    The unit won’t cool the apartment properly.
    Again, 13 SEER units operate at less pressure and a lower refrigerant temperature. Not all 13 SEER units will work properly
    with 10 SEER (or older) units.
