How Circuits Work
Current enters the apartment on “hot” wires (black or red).
Current returns to the service panel along “neutral” wires (white or light gray).
Current leaves the apartment on a large neutral service wire.
Grounding
Electricity always seeks to return to its source to complete a continuous circuit. In an apartment wiring system, electricity
returns by way of the white or light gray neutral wires that run to the service panel.
As a safety precaution, bare copper or insulated green “grounding” wires are used to conduct electricity if current tries to
return via a path other than the neutral wire. This prevents a short circuit . If a person touches a short-circuited electrical box,
tool or appliance, the electrical current may try to return to its source through the person’s body.
A grounding wire provides a safe, easy path for current to flow back to its source. It reduces the risk of receiving a severe
electrical shock from a short circuit.
Wiring systems are also required to be connected directly to the earth. The earth is able to absorb the electrons of electrical
current. In the event of a short circuit or overload, any extra electricity will follow the grounding wire to the earth, where it
cannot hurt anyone.
Grounding is completed by wiring the apartment building’s electrical system to metal grounding rods, which are buried in the
earth. In older buildings, cold water pipe may be used instead.
Overloaded Circuits
When a circuit tries to carry too much power—too many appliances plugged into a receptacle, for example—it becomes
overloaded. A fuse or circuit breaker protects each circuit in case of short circuits or overloads. A “blown fuse” or “tripped
circuit breaker” is usually the result of an overloaded circuit.
