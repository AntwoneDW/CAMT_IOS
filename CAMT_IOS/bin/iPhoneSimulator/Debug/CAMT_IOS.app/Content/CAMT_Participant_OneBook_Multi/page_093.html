Wall Switches
An electrical switch is a device designed to break the flow of electric current. When it’s
turned on, a switch completes the electrical circuit and current flows to the light or other
device the switch controls. When the switch is off, the electrical circuit is not complete,
denying power to whatever the switch controls.
Anatomy of a Wall Switch
Wall switches connect to circuit wires with screws or push-in terminals. The switch
body is attached to a metal mounting strap that allows it to be mounted to an
electrical box.
Look for the following information stamped into the metal:
  • UL or UND. Lab. INC.: Meets the safety standards of Underwriters
    • Laboratories.
  • Maximum voltage and amperage ratings: Standard is 15A 125V. Voltage
    • ratings of 110, 120, and 125 are considered to be identical for purposes of
    identification.
  • Wire type: COPPER or CU or CO/ALR.
  • Wire gauge rating: #12 or #14.
    • In addition, many switches have a stamped strip gauge that indicates how much
    insulation must be stripped from the circuit wires to make the connection.
