Key Parts of Hydronic Heating Systems
Hydronic heating systems have many configurations. Here are the most common
components.
Terminal Units
The system’s heat exchangers: radiators or sections of baseboard.
Boiler
A large tank that heats water with natural gas or oil providing the fuel.
Aquastat
The aquastat, like a thermostat in other systems, is a temperature-sensing device responsible for cycling the boiler on and off
to keep water at the desired temperature.
Low-water Cutoff
The low-water cutoff is a safety device that de-energizes the boiler if its water falls below a certain level.
Standard Expansion Tank/Extrol Tank
Water expands when it is heated. The expansion tank creates the extra space needed to hold the heated water.
Circulators
Circulators move water through the hydronic system. They are made up of a motor, a linkage, and an impeller, and they
operate on centrifugal force.
The impeller slaps against the water and throws it toward the outside of the housing where it is forced from the pump at a
higher pressure. The pressure increase from here pushes water around the piping circuit until it eventually returns.
Air Separators
Air is the enemy of the hydronic heating system. Air separators collect tiny air bubbles from water and pass them to the
automatic or manual air vents where they are removed.
Point of No Pressure Change
This is the one point in the piping system where the pump cannot affect the pressure in the system.
Pressure-Reducing Valve
This valve automatically drops the pressure of the water to the level at which the boiler was designed to operate. If the
pressure drops below a certain level, the valve opens to give the boiler more water.
