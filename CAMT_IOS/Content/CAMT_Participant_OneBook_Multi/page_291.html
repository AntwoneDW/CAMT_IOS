Hydronic Heating Systems (continued)
How Hydronic Heating Systems Work
Hydronic systems rely on circulating water to deliver heat to the locations where heating is desired. Instead of using large air
ducts, like furnaces, water is heated at one location and then pumped to other places via piping. The heat is transferred to the
space by heat exchangers located within the space. These heat exchangers are usually radiators or baseboards and are also
called terminal units.
After the water has passed through the terminal units, it is returned to the heat source, where the process repeats itself until
the space reaches the desired temperature.
There are many configurations for hydronic systems. We’ll cover the general information first, then ask you to describe your
specific experiences with it.
Heating Water
Before residents can enjoy hydronic heating, the water must be heated. There are a variety of ways to do this: burning fuel,
solar energy, electrical energy, or heat pumps. The most common method for heating water is burning a fuel such as natural
gas, manufactured gas, or oil.
The fuel heats the water in the boiler, which is usually made of cast iron or steel and divided into individual sections. The more
sections, the higher the capacity; there are more heat exchange surfaces between the water and burning fuel.
Due to the use of iron, the exposure to air, and the presence of water, regularly inspect the hydronic heating system for signs
of corrosion.
The Piping System
There are four types of piping configurations that hydronic heating systems use:
  • Series loop: All the heaters are piped in a series with each other. A drawback of this system is that the terminal units
    • receiving the water last are often at a lower temperature than those fed first.
  • One-pipe: One main piping loop extends around or under the space to be heated and connects the supply of the boiler
    • back to the boiler’s return.
  • Two-pipe direct return: Two pipes, one supply (carries the water to the terminal units) and the other return (brings the
    • water back to the boiler) work to feed water to the closest terminal unit and then return it to the boiler. Balancing valves are
    used to regulate the water to the other baseboard sections.
  • Two-pipe reverse return: Opposite of the two-pipe direct return system, the reverse return system supplies the water
    • first to the farthest baseboard section.
    One important thing to remember about piping is that return pipes must slope toward the boiler to work correctly.
