Evacuating and Dehydrating the System
In this part of the training, you’ll learn how to deal with two enemies of the refrigeration
system—excess air and moisture.
Introduction
When air and moisture get trapped in a refrigeration system, they’ll need to be evacuated with a vacuum pump. Air reduces
the amount of heat transfer and may cause a system to operate erratically. Moisture can freeze-up in the system and block
the flow of refrigerant; it can also combine with refrigerant and oil, creating sludge and acid.
Needless to say, all these conditions can damage the air conditioning system or cause it to fail.
How Evacuation Works
When an air conditioning system is evacuated, the vacuum pump creates a difference in pressure between the system and
the pump. This, in turn, causes the air and moisture trapped in the system at a higher pressure to move to a lower pressure
area created in the vacuum pump.
When the vacuum pump lowers the pressure in the system enough (determined by the ambient temperature of the system),
the trapped liquid moisture will boil and change into vapor. Then, just like air, this water vapor is pulled out of the system,
processed through the vacuum pump, and released to the atmosphere.
When to Evacuate a System
You’ll need to evacuate an air conditioning system anytime it has been opened to the atmosphere, for example, after fixing a
leak or replacing a part like a compressor.
Two Methods of Evacuation
  • The deep vacuum method is typically used making a repair that involved refrigerant recovery.
  • The triple evacuation method is typically recommended for especially “wet” systems.
    • Note: The process of evacuation is performed while there is NO refrigerant in a system. In other words, before
    the system starts up or after refrigerant recovery the vacuation needs to be performed.
